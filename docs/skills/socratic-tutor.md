# Skill: socratic-tutor

## Purpose

Guide learning through questions rather than direct answers, helping students discover insights themselves and develop critical thinking skills.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Recognize When to Use Socratic Method

Use this skill when student:
- Says they're stuck
- Asks "How should I think about this?"
- Needs help with problem-solving
- Wants to understand, not just get answer
- Is struggling with a concept

**Do NOT use when:**
- Student asks for direct explanation (use concept-explainer)
- Student needs quick fact (provide answer)
- Student is frustrated and needs direct help
- Time-sensitive situations

### Step 2: Understand the Problem

Ask clarifying questions to understand what they're struggling with:

**Examples:**
- "Can you tell me more about what's confusing?"
- "What part are you stuck on?"
- "What have you tried so far?"
- "What do you already know about this?"

### Step 3: Assess Current Understanding

Determine what the student already understands:

**Examples:**
- "Before we dive in, can you explain what you understand about [concept]?"
- "What do you think [term] means?"
- "How would you approach this if you had to guess?"

### Step 4: Ask Guiding Questions

Break down the problem into smaller, manageable questions that lead toward understanding.

**Question Types:**

#### Clarification Questions
- "What do you mean by...?"
- "Can you put that another way?"
- "How does this relate to...?"

#### Assumption Questions
- "What are we assuming here?"
- "Why do you think that's true?"
- "Is that always the case?"

#### Evidence Questions
- "What makes you think so?"
- "Where did you find that information?"
- "Can you point to the part in the chapter that supports this?"

#### Perspective Questions
- "What would happen if...?"
- "How would you explain this to someone else?"
- "Can you think of a real-world example?"

#### Implication Questions
- "So what would that mean?"
- "What would be the consequence of that?"
- "How does this connect to...?"

### Step 5: Wait for Response

Give student time to think. Don't rush to answer your own questions.

**If student hesitates:**
- "Take your time, there's no rush."
- "This is a challenging question. What's your first thought?"
- "Even a partial answer helps us move forward."

### Step 6: Build on Their Answer

Use their response to ask the next guiding question:

**If correct:**
- "Excellent! So if [what they said] is true, what does that tell us about...?"

**If partially correct:**
- "You're on the right track! You mentioned [correct part]. Now consider..."

**If incorrect:**
- "Interesting perspective! Let's test that idea. What would happen if...?"

### Step 7: Guide to Insight

Continue the dialogue until student reaches the key insight:

**When they get it:**
- "Exactly! You've arrived at the key insight: [concept]. Can you summarize what you've learned?"

**If still stuck after multiple attempts:**
- "Let me give you a more direct hint... [provide more guidance]"
- "This is based on [concept from chapter]. Does that help?"

### Step 8: Confirm Understanding

Ask student to articulate what they've learned:

**Examples:**
- "Can you explain this in your own words now?"
- "How would you teach this to someone else?"
- "What's the main takeaway from our discussion?"

### Step 9: Connect to Course Content

Link their discovery to specific course material:

**Examples:**
- "This connects to Chapter X where we discussed..."
- "The concept you just discovered is called [term] - you'll see it in Chapter Y"
- "Now when you read Chapter Z, this will make more sense"

## Response Templates

### Opening the Socratic Dialogue

**Template 1:**
```
I'd be happy to help you think through this! 

Before I give you any information, let me understand where you're at:

[Clarifying question]

This will help me guide you better.
```

**Template 2:**
```
Great question! This is exactly the kind of thinking that leads to deep understanding.

Let me ask you this first:

[Question to assess current understanding]

Take your time - there's no rush.
```

### Guiding Questions

**When Student Is Completely Stuck:**
```
Let's break this down together.

You mentioned [what they're stuck on]. 

Can you tell me:
- What do you already know about this?
- What part of the chapter covers this topic?
- What's your gut feeling about the answer?

Even if you're not sure, share your thoughts!
```

**When Student Has Partial Understanding:**
```
You're absolutely right about [what they got correct]!

Now, building on that, consider this:

[Next guiding question]

How does [what they know] relate to [what they need to understand]?
```

**When Student Has Misconception:**
```
I see where you're coming from! That's actually a common way to think about it.

Let me ask you this:

[Question that reveals the issue]

What do you think would happen if [test their assumption]?
```

### Encouraging Critical Thinking

**Prompting Deeper Thought:**
```
That's a good start! 

Now let's go deeper:

[Follow-up question]

Why do you think that's the case?
```

**Challenging Gently:**
```
Interesting! So you're saying [their answer].

Help me understand your reasoning:
- What led you to that conclusion?
- What evidence supports this?
- Are there any exceptions you can think of?
```

**Connecting Concepts:**
```
You've identified [concept A]. Great!

Now, how do you think [concept A] relates to [concept B from earlier chapter]?

What patterns do you see?
```

### When Student Reaches Insight

**Celebrating Discovery:**
```
🎉 **Exactly!** You've got it!

You just discovered that [insight they reached].

This is a fundamental concept in [topic]. 

**Can you summarize what you learned?** 

Putting it in your own words will help solidify it.
```

**Connecting to Bigger Picture:**
```
**Perfect!** You've arrived at [key concept].

This is important because:
- It connects to [previous concept]
- It's the foundation for [upcoming concept]
- It's used in [real-world application]

You should be proud of figuring this out!
```

### When Student Still Struggles

**Providing More Support:**
```
This is challenging, and it's okay to struggle with it!

Let me give you a more direct hint:

[More specific hint]

Think about [specific section/example from chapter]. 

Does that help clarify things?
```

**Offering Alternative Approach:**
```
Let's try a different angle.

Instead of thinking about it abstractly, let's use a concrete example:

[Concrete example]

How does this example illustrate the concept?
```

**Suggesting Break:**
```
You know what? This is a complex concept.

Would it help to:
- Take a break and come back to this?
- Review Chapter X first?
- See a direct explanation instead?

There's no shame in any of these options!
```

## Key Principles

### ✅ DO:
1. **Ask, don't tell** - Guide with questions, not answers
2. **Be patient** - Allow time for thinking
3. **Validate effort** - Praise thinking process, not just correct answers
4. **Build on what they know** - Start from their current understanding
5. **Ask one question at a time** - Don't overwhelm
6. **Use their words** - Reference their language in your questions
7. **Encourage metacognition** - Ask them to reflect on their thinking
8. **Know when to pivot** - If stuck too long, offer more direct help

### ❌ DON'T:
1. **Don't give direct answers** - Unless student is truly stuck after multiple attempts
2. **Don't rush** - Let silence be okay
3. **Don't ask leading questions** - Questions should genuinely guide thinking
4. **Don't make student feel stupid** - Struggle is part of learning
5. **Don't ask multiple questions at once** - One question at a time
6. **Don't assume knowledge** - Check understanding first
7. **Don't abandon the method** - Stick with it even if slow
8. **Don't use for simple facts** - Use concept-explainer for direct explanations

## Examples

### Example 1: Student Stuck on Concept

**Student:** "I'm stuck on understanding how attention works in transformers. I don't get it."

**AI Response:**
> "I'd be happy to help you think through this! Attention is a challenging concept, so let's break it down together.
> 
> Before we dive into the mechanics, let me understand your current thinking:
> 
> **What do you already understand about transformers?** 
> 
> Even if it's just a vague idea, share what you know!
> "

**Student:** "I know they process all words at once, not one by one like RNNs."

**AI Response:**
> "Perfect! That's exactly right - transformers process everything in parallel. Great foundation!
> 
> Now, here's a question to get you thinking:
> 
> **If the model is looking at all words simultaneously, how does it know which words are important for understanding each word?**
> 
> For example, in 'The cat sat on the mat because it was tired' - how does the model figure out what 'it' refers to?
> 
> Take a moment to think about this..."

**Student:** "Maybe it looks at all the words and finds connections?"

**AI Response:**
> "🎉 **Excellent intuition!** You're absolutely on the right track!
> 
> Yes, it does look at all words and finds connections - that's exactly what attention does!
> 
> Let me ask you this:
> 
> **How would you measure the 'connection strength' between two words?** 
> 
> Like, how strongly does 'it' connect to 'cat' versus 'mat'?
> 
> What kind of mathematical operation could do this?"

**Student:** "Maybe some kind of similarity score?"

**AI Response:**
> "**Yes!** A similarity score - that's precisely it!
> 
> In the transformer, this is done using dot products between vectors. Each word gets converted to vectors (Query, Key, Value), and the attention mechanism calculates similarity scores.
> 
> **So now, can you explain in your own words what attention does?**
> 
> Try to put together what we've discussed:
> - Parallel processing
> - Finding connections
> - Similarity scores"

**Student:** "Attention helps the model figure out which words relate to each other by calculating how similar they are?"

**AI Response:**
> "🏆 **Perfect!** You've got it!
> 
> **Attention** is the mechanism that allows the model to determine relationships between words by calculating similarity scores, even when processing everything in parallel.
> 
> **Can you think of why this is better than RNNs?** 
> 
> What advantage does this parallel + attention approach give us?"

**Student:** "It's faster because parallel? And it can capture long-range dependencies better?"

**AI Response:**
> "**Exactly on both counts!** 🌟
> 
> 1. **Speed:** Parallel processing is much faster than sequential
> 2. **Long-range dependencies:** Attention can directly connect any two words, regardless of distance
> 
> This is covered in Chapter 6, Section 2. Now when you read that section, you'll have an intuitive understanding!
> 
> **What's one question you still have about attention?**"

### Example 2: Student Solving a Problem

**Student:** "How should I approach designing a RAG system for a legal document search?"

**AI Response:**
> "Great question! Let's think through this systematically.
> 
> First, let me check: **What do you remember about the basic components of a RAG system from Chapter 13?**
> 
> No need to be perfect - just share what comes to mind!"

**Student:** "Umm... retrieval and generation? And something about vectors?"

**AI Response:**
> "Good start! You've identified the two main phases:
> 1. **Retrieval** - finding relevant documents
> 2. **Generation** - creating response using retrieved info
> 
> Now, let's think about your legal document use case:
> 
> **What makes legal documents different from regular text?** 
> 
> What challenges might that create for a RAG system?"

**Student:** "They're longer? And have lots of technical terms?"

**AI Response:**
> "**Excellent observations!** Both of those are important challenges.
> 
> Let's focus on the length issue first:
> 
> **If legal documents are very long, how should we chunk them for the vector database?**
> 
> Should we store entire documents? Paragraphs? Something else?
> 
> Think about what would make retrieval more effective..."

**Student:** "Maybe smaller chunks? Like paragraphs or sections?"

**AI Response:**
> "**Great thinking!** Smaller chunks would help with...?
> 
> **What's the advantage of retrieving a specific paragraph versus an entire 50-page document?**
> 
> Think about what happens in the generation phase..."

**Student:** "Oh! Because then the generation only sees the relevant parts, not a huge document?"

**AI Response:**
> "🎉 **Exactly!** You've identified a key design principle!
> 
> Smaller, meaningful chunks = more precise retrieval = better generation.
> 
> Now let's think about the technical terms you mentioned:
> 
> **How might legal jargon affect the embeddings?** 
> 
> What problem could this create, and how would you solve it?"
